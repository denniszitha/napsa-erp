Getting Started with Risk Management

What is Risk Management?

Risk management is the process of identifying, assessing, and controlling threats to an organization's capital and earnings¹. These risks stem from various sources including financial uncertainties, legal liabilities, technology issues, strategic management errors, accidents, and natural disasters.

¹ Note: NAPSA follows the ISO 31000:2018 Risk Management Guidelines and COSO ERM Framework for comprehensive risk management.

How to Create a New Risk

1 Navigate to the Risk Register by clicking Risk Management in the sidebar

2 Click the "Add New Risk" button in the top-right corner

3 Fill in the required fields:

  • Risk Title: A brief, descriptive name for the risk²
  • Description: Detailed explanation of the risk and its potential impact
  • Category: Select from predefined categories Operational, Financial, Compliance, Strategic, Reputational
  • Department: The department primarily affected by this risk
  • Risk Owner: Person responsible for managing this risk

4 Assess the initial risk level using the likelihood and impact scales Both use a 1-5 scale where 1 is lowest and 5 is highest

5 Click "Save Risk" to add it to the register

² Best Practice: Risk titles should be clear and specific. Example: "Data Breach Due to Inadequate Cybersecurity" rather than just "Security Risk".

Likelihood Scale:
  • 1 - Very Unlikely: Less than 5% chance per year
  • 2 - Unlikely: 5-25% chance per year
  • 3 - Possible: 25-50% chance per year
  • 4 - Likely: 50-75% chance per year
  • 5 - Very Likely: More than 75% chance per year
Impact Scale:
  • 1 - Minimal: Minor impact, less than ZMW 1M
  • 2 - Minor: Limited impact, ZMW 1-5M
  • 3 - Moderate: Significant impact, ZMW 5-20M
  • 4 - Major: Major disruption, ZMW 20-50M
  • 5 - Severe: Critical impact, over ZMW 50M
³ Important: Both inherent risk (before controls) and residual risk (after controls) should be assessed. The difference shows control effectiveness.

Managing Risk Controls

Types of Controls

Preventive Controls: Stop risks from occurring⁴

  • Access controls and authentication
  • Segregation of duties
  • Authorization limits

Detective Controls: Identify when risks occur

  • Monitoring and alerts
  • Reconciliations
  • Performance reviews

Corrective Controls: Fix issues after occurrence

  • Incident response procedures
  • Business continuity plans
  • Recovery processes

Linking Controls to Risks

  1. Select a risk from the Risk Register
  2. Click "Manage Controls" button
  3. Choose existing controls or create new ones
  4. Assess control effectiveness (0-100%)
  5. Save the control mapping
⁴ Control Effectiveness: Regularly test controls to ensure they're working as designed. Document testing results and remediation actions.

Risk Treatment Plans

Four Treatment Strategies (The 4 T's)

1. Treat (Mitigate): Reduce the risk through controls⁵

2. Transfer: Share risk with third parties (insurance, outsourcing)

3. Tolerate (Accept): Accept the risk within appetite levels

4. Terminate (Avoid): Eliminate the risk by stopping the activity

Creating a Treatment Plan

  1. Identify high-priority risks needing treatment
  2. Select appropriate treatment strategy
  3. Define specific actions and timelines
  4. Assign responsible parties
  5. Estimate costs and expected risk reduction
  6. Monitor implementation progress
⁵ Decision Criteria: Consider cost-benefit analysis, risk appetite, regulatory requirements, and organizational capabilities when selecting treatment strategies.

Risk Reporting

Standard Risk Reports

  • Risk Register Report: Complete list of all identified risks
  • Risk Heat Map: Visual representation of risk distribution
  • Top Risks Report: Focus on highest-scoring risks
  • Risk Movement Report: Changes in risk levels over time
  • Control Effectiveness Report: Assessment of control performance
  • Treatment Progress Report: Status of risk treatment plans

Generating Reports

  1. Navigate to Reports section
  2. Select report type
  3. Choose date range and filters
  4. Click "Generate Report"
  5. Export to PDF, Excel, or CSV format

Frequently Asked Questions

Risk reviews should be conducted:

  • High risks: Monthly review
  • Medium risks: Quarterly review
  • Low risks: Annual review
  • After significant events: Immediate review

Risk management permissions:

  • Risk Managers: Full create, edit, delete permissions
  • Department Heads: Create and edit risks for their department
  • Risk Owners: Edit risks assigned to them
  • Viewers: Read-only access to risk register

Inherent Risk: The level of risk before any controls are applied.

Residual Risk: The remaining risk level after controls are implemented.

The difference between these two values indicates the effectiveness of your controls.
